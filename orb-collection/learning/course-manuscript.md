# 1) ORB Basics

## What is ORB?

ORB means **Opening Range Breakout**.

It uses the high and low of the first part of the market day.
If price breaks above the range, that is a possible long setup.
If price breaks below the range, that is a possible short setup.

## Core idea

1. Mark the opening range high and low.
2. Wait for breakout direction.
3. Confirm with volume and price behavior.
4. Enter with a stop loss.
5. Exit using a target plan.

## Basic setup definitions

| Setup | What you look for |
|---|---|
| Long ORB | Price breaks above range high and holds |
| Short ORB | Price breaks below range low and holds |
| False breakout | Price breaks out, then quickly fails back inside range |

## Long and short entry rules (simple)

### Long entry

- Price closes above opening range high.
- Breakout bar volume is at or above recent average.
- Optional safer entry: wait for retest of range high and bounce.

### Short entry

- Price closes below opening range low.
- Breakout bar volume is at or above recent average.
- Optional safer entry: wait for retest of range low and rejection.

# 2) Timeframe Playbooks

## Quick comparison

| Timeframe | Opening range window | Typical use | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| 5m | 09:30–09:35 | Fast entries | More noise, faster decisions |
| 15m | 09:30–09:45 | Beginner-friendly base model | Balanced speed and quality |
| 30m | 09:30–10:00 | Higher confirmation | Fewer but often cleaner setups |
| 60m | 09:30–10:30 | IB-style context trades | Slowest, most selective |

## Learning path by timeframe

1. Learn 15m first for 2+ weeks.
2. Add 30m to improve patience and filtering.
3. Add 5m only after you can follow rules without emotion.
4. Add 60m for context and selective high-quality setups.

## Playbook format

1. Mark OR high and OR low when the window closes.
2. Wait for a breakout close.
3. Check breakout volume.
4. Enter on close or on retest.
5. Place stop loss at the opposite side of setup structure.
6. Take profit using fixed R targets (1R, 2R, etc.).

## Per-timeframe notes

- **5m:** fast, noisy, strict stops.
- **15m:** default beginner model.
- **30m:** cleaner structure, fewer trades.
- **60m:** context-driven, selective.

# 3) Risk and Trade Management

## Stop loss

Your stop loss is the price that proves your trade idea is wrong.

### Long example

- OR high = 42,100
- Long entry = 42,108
- Stop below breakout/retest structure = 42,094
- Risk = 14 points

### Short example

- OR low = 41,980
- Short entry = 41,972
- Stop above breakout/retest structure = 41,986
- Risk = 14 points

## Take profit and R:R examples

| Target type | Formula (long example) | Result if risk = 14 points |
|---|---|---|
| 1R | Entry + 1 × risk | 42,122 |
| 2R | Entry + 2 × risk | 42,136 |
| 3R | Entry + 3 × risk | 42,150 |

Tip: beginners can scale out at 1R and hold a smaller runner for 2R+.

## Position sizing basics

1. Pick max dollar risk per trade.
2. Find stop distance in points.
3. Convert points to dollar risk per contract.
4. Contracts = max dollar risk ÷ risk per contract.

If result is not a whole number, round down.

## False breakout response

- Exit quickly if invalidation is hit.
- Do not widen stop loss.
- Wait for a new valid setup.
